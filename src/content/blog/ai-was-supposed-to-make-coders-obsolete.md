If you've spent any time online lately, you've absorbed the storyline by now: **AI is coming for the programmers first**.

The robots can write code, so the thinking goes, and once they get good enough, the humans get shown the door. Layoff headlines roll in, CEOs talk about "leaner teams," and everyone nods along to the inevitable.

But dig into what's actually happening and that story falls apart fast.

**Software engineering** is the perfect place to test the "AI is eating the jobs" theory. It's the field where AI is most capable, where it's been adopted fastest, and where almost nothing regulatory is slowing it down.

If mass replacement were going to happen anywhere, it would happen here first. It isn't. So it's worth pulling apart what people believe from what the evidence shows.

## Myth 1: Companies are laying off engineers because AI replaced them

A lot of those "**AI layoffs**" are just rebranded budget cuts.

Call it "AI washing," the corporate cousin of greenwashing. When a company needs to trim payroll, "we're harnessing AI to build smaller, sharper teams" sounds a lot better to investors than "we overhired and now we're bleeding cash."

Three of the splashiest examples:

- **Block**, the company behind Cash App and Square, [cut around 4,000 jobs](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/jack-dorsey-s-4-000-job-cuts-at-block-arouse-suspicions-of-ai-washing), with Jack Dorsey pointing to [AI advances](https://www.wired.com/story/jack-dorsey-explains-block-layoffs/). But it had roughly tripled its headcount during the pandemic and was under serious financial strain. One data scientist there said AI had been ["shoved down everyone's throats"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/naoko-takeda_my-employer-block-laid-off-40-of-its-workforce-activity-7434699479389405184--D3P) with "very limited gains in productivity," and walked away from a 75% retention raise rather than stay.
- **Snap** [laid off about 1,000 people](https://deadline.com/2026/04/snap-layoffs-ceo-evan-spiegel-ai-1236861335/) while its CEO bragged that AI was writing [65% of new code](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/snap-ceo-praises-ai-writing-090500386.html). The less glamorous backstory: [an activist investor had been demanding cost cuts](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260331059373/en/Irenic-Sends-Letter-to-Snap-Inc.-Co-Founder-and-CEO-Evan-Spiegel-and-Issues-Presentation-Outlining-Actionable-Steps-to-Unlock-Value), and Snap has posted a loss every year since going public in 2017.
- **Intuit** [trimmed 3,000 roles](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/20/intuit-to-lay-off-over-3000-employees-to-refocus-on-ai/) right around the time it signed deals with major AI labs, and the press ran with the obvious narrative. Except the CEO [pushed back](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/20/intuit-ceo-says-companys-17percent-workforce-cut-had-nothing-to-do-with-ai.html), saying the cuts had nothing to do with AI and everything to do with too many layers of management.

![Executives presenting AI layoffs while budget spreadsheets and cost-cutting notes sit in the background](../../assets/images/ai-layoffs-vs-reality.jpeg)

The numbers back the skepticism.

In one survey, [59% of hiring managers admitted](https://www.resumetemplates.com/the-great-turnover-9-in-10-companies-plan-to-hire-in-2026-yet-6-in-10-will-have-layoffs-2/) they lean on the AI explanation because it plays better with stakeholders than the truth. A Forrester analyst found that [nine in ten companies prepping "AI-driven" layoffs](https://hrexecutive.com/the-truth-behind-ai-driven-layoffs-90-of-companies-arent-ready/) don't even have a working AI tool ready to do the jobs they're cutting.

And a [Harvard Business Review survey](https://hbr.org/2026/01/companies-are-laying-off-workers-because-of-ais-potential-not-its-performance) of more than 1,000 executives turned up a telling gap: 21% had made big layoffs in anticipation of AI, but only 2% had done so because AI had actually taken over the work.

Ten times as much fear as fact.

The clearest evidence is in New York. In 2025 the state became the first to add an ["is this layoff because of AI?" checkbox](https://www.hunton.com/hunton-employment-labor-perspectives/new-york-warn-act-no-ai-related-layoffs-reported-in-first-year-of-adding-ai-related-disclosure-to-the-system) to its required layoff filings.

In the first full year, more than 160 companies filed, and exactly one, Nespresso, checked the box. That works out to roughly 46 of about 25,000 laid-off workers, or 0.2%.

And if AI were genuinely making teams more productive, mass firings wouldn't even be the smart play.

Firing people torches institutional knowledge and costs a fortune in severance and morale. Real AI impact shows up as [slower hiring, not pink slips](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5425555), and the [Federal Reserve's economists](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/ai-and-coder-employment-compiling-the-evidence.htm) put that slowdown at around three percentage points a year.

Real, but a long way from an apocalypse.

## Myth 2: Once AI writes all the code, you won't need engineers

Writing code was never the hard part.

This is the myth that powers all the others, and it rests on a faulty mental model. Executives love quoting "what percent of our code is written by AI," as if hitting 100% makes coders unnecessary.

But that number is close to meaningless, because typing out code was never the bottleneck.

A [Microsoft study of 6,000 developers](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/devtime-preprint-TSE19.pdf) found they spend somewhere between 9% and 61% of their time actually writing code. The rest goes to figuring out what to build, debugging, coordinating, and making sure the thing works.

When developers handed the typing over to AI agents, many were surprised how little their overall output changed.

To see why, picture a restaurant kitchen instead of a factory.

## The chef's kitchen

Running a kitchen breaks into three stages.

1. **Planning the menu.** The head chef decides what to serve, weighing the season's ingredients, the budget, dietary needs, health codes, and what the restaurant is trying to be. This is judgment-heavy, deeply human work. A machine can't know that your Friday regular is gluten-intolerant and likes things extra spicy.
2. **Cooking the food.** The chopping, the searing, the simmering. This is the repetitive labor, and it's exactly what a high-speed food processor, or in our case AI, can blast through at incredible speed.
3. **Tasting and plating.** Before any dish leaves the kitchen, someone tastes it, adjusts the seasoning, checks the presentation, and takes responsibility for the fact that it's safe to eat. The chef owns that plate. No restaurant lets a robot send dishes out to paying customers unsupervised, because the stakes are too high.

What AI does is turbocharge the middle stage. The cooking gets faster and cheaper. The menu planning and the tasting barely budge.

So the kitchen gets far more efficient at the part that was never really the problem.

There's hard data for this. A [study tracking 100,000 developers on GitHub](https://www.nber.org/papers/w35275) found that AI agents helped them write eight times more lines of code but produced only 30% more actual releases.

All that extra cooking, and the menu planning and quality control still set the pace.

These stages aren't a strict sequence, by the way. A chef bounces between planning, cooking, and tasting constantly, re-tweaking a recipe mid-service. Engineers work the same way.

And underneath all of it sits the chef's palate: years of know-how about how flavors interact, how the equipment behaves, how to read the room.

That's the deep understanding of the codebase and the business that makes the other two stages possible, and you can't outsource it to a gadget.

So why won't planning and tasting get automated away?

**Planning resists automation** because it's about judgment: what users need, where the market is going, which business priorities win, what the regulations allow.

As AI starts handling the simpler decisions, the valuable ones just float upward to the humans. And since software keeps getting more complex with no ceiling in sight, there's always more high-level judgment to do.

**Tasting and delivery resist automation** because someone has to be accountable.

Today's AI is too unreliable to ship mission-critical work without a human signing off. Even when it gets more reliable, we can choose to keep humans on the hook through liability law and professional norms.

We don't let robots send out plates unchecked, and we won't let them ship hospital software unchecked either.

This squeeze isn't even new. U.S. labor statistics have distinguished "programmers" from "software engineers" for over two decades.

The pure-programming roles, the cooking-only jobs, have been shrinking and paying less the whole time. AI is just speeding up a trend that's been running for years.

What about the viral horror stories, like the [AI agent that wiped a production database](https://fortune.com/2025/07/23/ai-coding-tool-replit-wiped-database-called-it-a-catastrophic-failure/)?

Those are man-bites-dog stories. They go viral because they're rare and reckless, not because they're the new normal. If it made the news, you probably don't need to worry about it.

## Myth 3: "Vibe coding" proves anyone can build software now

Letting a stranger dump ingredients in a blender isn't the same as a chef using one.

You've probably heard of "**vibe coding**": telling an AI what you want, accepting whatever it spits out, and never really looking under the hood.

People do build cute little apps this way. But there's a wide gap between that and what professionals actually do.

Think of it as two ends of a spectrum:

| Dimension      | Vibe coding                                                     | Professional AI-assisted engineering                             |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| AI's role      | The AI does the work while the human mostly accepts or retries. | The AI speeds up the work while the human keeps steering.        |
| Best fit       | Throwaway tools, demos, prototypes, and personal experiments.   | Production software that other people will depend on.            |
| Who can do it  | Anyone can try it, which is part of the appeal.                 | Engineers with enough judgment to know when the output is wrong. |
| Specification  | Vague prompts and "keep regenerating until it looks right."     | Clear requirements, constraints, tests, and acceptance criteria. |
| Understanding  | Little or no mental model of the codebase required.             | A working model of the architecture, data flow, and edge cases.  |
| Verification   | Run it, eyeball it, and ask the AI to patch whatever breaks.    | Tests, review, debugging, guardrails, and pushback.              |
| Maintenance    | Usually not a concern. Version control may not even exist.      | Reviewable commits, refactoring discipline, and clean history.   |
| Security       | Often ignored until something obvious breaks.                   | Treated as part of the job, with review and automated checks.    |
| Accountability | Low stakes. Nobody serious is on the hook.                      | High stakes. A human still owns the outcome.                     |
| Skill effect   | Passive acceptance can make weak skills weaker.                 | Good workflows preserve skill and can make engineers sharper.    |

![Split view of chaotic vibe coding on the left and a professional engineer supervising AI tools on the right](../../assets/images/vibe-coding-vs-professional-engineering.jpeg)

The data on unsupervised AI use is rough. In one [dataset of coding-agent interactions](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.20779), only 44% of the code the agents produced survived into actual commits.

Most of it got thrown out.

Worse, the vibe-coded work introduced security vulnerabilities at nine times the rate of human-written code.

One detail I love: the most common reason people reached for these agents wasn't generating new code at all. It was understanding code that already existed.

And supervising the AI is genuinely hard work.

The developer Simon Willison has [described being mentally fried by 11 a.m.](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/an-ai-state-of-the-union) just from babysitting his agents. That's not a job vanishing. It's a job turning into something else.

The engineer of the future looks less like a typist and more like a crane operator: not lifting the load by hand, but skillfully running the machine that does, and staying responsible for where it lands.

![A software engineer in a control room supervising AI coding agents before approving a release](../../assets/images/ai-agent-supervision.jpeg)

No company ships real production software by swapping skilled engineers for unqualified vibe coders. It just doesn't work.

## Myth 4: Even if jobs are safe now, they're doomed long-term

Cheaper software might mean we need more engineers, not fewer.

It sounds backwards, but when something gets cheaper to make, people often buy a lot more of it. Economists call this [**Jevons' paradox**](https://fortune.com/2026/04/28/will-ai-kill-jobs-why-not-jevons-paradox-torsten-slok/), and software responds to price this way in spades.

![Cheaper AI-powered software production leading to rising demand across apps, devices, and industries](../../assets/images/jevons-paradox.jpeg)

Make it cheaper to build and the world's appetite for software balloons. Since AI lowers the cost of building without replacing the people who do the planning and quality control, more software could mean demand for more engineers, not fewer.

History backs this up. Programmers went from basically zero in 1950 to millions today, even as the tools got dramatically better the whole way.

A [modern car runs on something like 100 million lines of code](https://spectrum.ieee.org/this-car-runs-on-code). We're nowhere near the ceiling, if there even is one.

Compare farming, where mechanization gutted the labor force because people can only eat so many calories. Software has no such limit.

The new jobs may just sit in different places: more engineers embedded inside ordinary, non-tech businesses, maybe even at firms bought up specifically to be rebuilt ["AI-native"](https://www.generalcatalyst.com/stories/the-future-of-services).

And for anyone insisting AI will finally "democratize" coding so non-programmers build everything themselves, we've heard this exact pitch before.

FORTRAN, COBOL, and SQL were each supposed to make professional programmers obsolete. The [same hope surrounded SQL](https://cacm.acm.org/research/50-years-of-queries/), and it did not happen, because the real barrier was never the syntax. It was the judgment and accountability the job demands.

Forty years ago, the computing pioneer Fred Brooks made the same point in ["No Silver Bullet"](https://www.cs.unc.edu/techreports/86-020.pdf): better tools chip away at the accidental difficulty of building software, but the essential difficulty, figuring out exactly what the thing should do, stays stubbornly hard.

AI is no exception.

## So where does that leave us?

![A confident chef-engineer in a kitchen full of fast AI tools, still tasting the final dish before it leaves the pass](../../assets/images/ai-coders-conclusion.jpeg)

Cautiously optimistic.

The "AI is about to wipe out software engineers" story doesn't survive contact with the evidence.

AI is genuinely great at the cooking, the part that was never the real constraint, while the menu planning and the tasting stay in human hands, partly because AI can't do them well and partly because we'll choose to keep people accountable.

A healthy job market overall doesn't mean every individual engineer sails through untouched.

The ground is shifting, and who comes out ahead will depend on seniority, location, the kind of employer you work for, and how fast you adapt.

But that's a story about turbulence and reinvention, not robots quietly taking everyone's badge on the way out.

The chef isn't getting fired. The kitchen's just getting a very fast new gadget.
